About North Central Little League

NCLL sits at the intersection of community history, Lower Woodland's Cloverleaf fields, and generations of volunteers dedicated to helping kids fall in love with baseball and softball.

The Cloverleaf

The Cloverleaf at Lower Woodland is more than a cluster of diamonds—it is the main stage for every North Central player. As kids move from Tee to Teen, the bleachers swell with families, coaches pour countless hours into practices, and the soundtrack of the season echoes across those fields.

Because that space is woven into our identity, we refreshed the NCLL badge and, in the spring 2025 brand refresh, introduced the new clover mark to lean fully into its shape and energy. Spotting the clover on gear or uniforms now feels like carrying a piece of the Lower Woodland home fields wherever you go. Explore the full details on our logo refresh page.

Who We Are

North Central Little League (NCLL) serves families across Green Lake, Greenwood, Phinney Ridge, Fremont, Wallingford, and Ballard—offering baseball and softball opportunities for players at every experience level.

We emphasize skill growth, teamwork, inclusion, and community connection. Volunteer coaches and families create a supportive environment where players learn the game, gain confidence, and simply enjoy being part of a team.

Green Lake & Lower Woodland Fields

Lower Woodland’s athletic fields trace back to Guy C. Phinney’s late-1800s estate layout. After Seattle acquired the land in 1899, the Olmsted Brothers’ 1910 plan formalized the park’s expanded athletic use.

Shoreline changes beginning in 1911 opened additional playable space, and by 1913 a third field was in place. Later additions—including grandstands (1915) and a shelter with dressing rooms (1929)—show how quickly the community embraced the grounds. Today’s baseball and softball diamonds rest on more than a century of neighborhood sport; you can explore the complex on Google Maps.

Field Development Timeline

  • Late 1880s – Two private estate fields laid out by Guy C. Phinney
  • 1899 – City of Seattle acquires the property (city archives)
  • 1910 – Olmsted Brothers plan formalizes public athletic expansion (Olmsted legacy)
  • 1911 – Shoreline lowering & fill work begins (Green Lake history)
  • 1913 – Third athletic field created
  • 1915 – Second grandstand added
  • 1929 – Shelter with dressing rooms built; upper field integrated into the zoo
  • Today – Multi-diamond complex supporting youth baseball and softball
